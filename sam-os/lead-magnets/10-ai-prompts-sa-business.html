Quick Win 1

Write a week of social captions in 10 minutes

The problem it solves

You spend Sunday nights staring at a blank screen trying to come up with posts for the week. By Wednesday you've run out of ideas and end up sharing a meme that has nothing to do with your business.

Copy this prompt

I run a [type of business] in [city/suburb, SA]. My target customer is [describe your customer]. I sell [product/service]. I want you to write 7 social media captions for this week (Monday to Sunday). Each caption must: - Be under 150 characters - Solve one specific problem my customer has - End with a question or a call to action - Sound like a real person, not a marketing bot Topics for each day: Monday: [topic] Tuesday: [topic] Wednesday: [topic] Thursday: [topic] Friday: [topic] Saturday: [topic] Sunday: [topic] Write them in a table with columns: Day, Caption, Best time to post in SA.

What you'll get

Example for a Jozi baker:

Monday: "Your Monday deserves fresh croissants. We baked a batch at 5am — they're still warm. Grab yours before the morning rush takes them." (post 7am)

Wednesday: "Load shedding Stage 4 and your kids are hungry. Our pre-ordered lunchboxes don't need electricity. Just a smile when you collect." (post 10am)

Friday: "Three words: Friday. Afternoon. Scones. Last batch comes out at 3pm. First come, first served." (post 1pm)
Quick Win 2

Respond to customer enquiries on WhatsApp in bulk

The problem it solves

You get 30+ WhatsApp messages a day. "Do you have this in stock?" "How much?" "Can you deliver to [suburb]?" You're typing the same answers over and over.

Copy this prompt

I run a [type of business] in SA. I get a lot of WhatsApp enquiries asking the same things. Write me 10 ready-to-use WhatsApp reply templates (each under 200 characters) for these situations: 1. "Do you have [product] in stock?" (with and without stock) 2. "How much does [service] cost?" (give a price range) 3. "Do you deliver to [suburb]?" (yes/no with area list) 4. "What are your hours?" (include load shedding adjustment) 5. "Can I get a discount?" (polite refusal) 6. "How do I pay?" (include EFT and PayFast options) 7. "When can you start?" (give timeline with realistic buffer) 8. "Can you send me a quote?" (capture their email/WhatsApp) 9. "Do you do [specific request you don't offer]?" (referral) 10. "I'm ready to buy. What's next?" (clear next step) Tone: friendly but efficient. Leave blanks like [suburb] where I need to fill in.

What you'll get

Real case: A Johannesburg financial advisory firm deployed an AI assistant to handle routine client inquiries. Within 6 weeks, 60% of inbound queries were automated. Customer satisfaction scores rose 28%. Their team went from answering the same questions all day to focusing on complex client work.

Example reply: "Sure! You can EFT to [bank name], account [number], or pay instantly via PayFast [link]. Once payment reflects, I'll confirm within 30 minutes."

Why this works for SA

More SA customers message businesses on WhatsApp than call or email. Having 10 pre-written replies saves hours a day and makes you look professional even during load shedding when you're on mobile data.

Quick Win 3

Tell customers what's back in stock (without sounding spammy)

The problem it solves

You finally got stock after two weeks of "sorry, we're out." You need to tell your regulars without blasting them with a generic "SALE SALE SALE" message.

Copy this prompt

I own a [type of business] in [city, SA]. The following items are back in stock after being out for [duration]: [List items with brief details] Write 3 WhatsApp broadcast messages I can send to my customer list: 1. A "back in stock" message for regular customers (warm, appreciative) 2. A "limited stock" message for people who asked about it (personalised feel) 3. A one-liner for my WhatsApp status (under 100 characters) Each message must: - Sound like a real person, not a business - Include a call to action ("reply to order", "first come first served", etc.) - NOT use ALL CAPS, multiple exclamation marks, or emojis - Feel like a favour, not a sales pitch Also include a 30-character WhatsApp status version.

What you'll get

Real case: A Cape Town retail chain with 8 locations implemented AI predictive inventory. Within 4 months, excess inventory dropped 32%, saving the business over R350,000 in the first quarter. The system analysed sales trends and local seasonal patterns to predict what would sell — and what wouldn't.

Example message: "Hi [name], you asked about the rattan baskets a few weeks ago. We got a small batch in this morning. First come on collection — I'll hold one for you if you reply by 2pm."

Why this works for SA

SA runs on WhatsApp broadcasts, not email newsletters. The tone mirrors the friendly, transactional relationship local customers expect — not loud American-style sales language.

Quick Win 4

Chase late payments without losing the client

The problem it solves

You invoiced them three weeks ago. They've read your WhatsApp messages. You're stuck between "I need that money" and "I don't want to seem desperate."

Copy this prompt

I'm owed R[amount] by a client who is [number] days late on payment. Our relationship is [good / strained / professional / friendly]. I've already sent [number] reminders. I need you to write 4 messages I can send in sequence: 1. A gentle nudge (assume they forgot) 2. A firmer follow-up (day 5 after first message) 3. A "let's sort this out" message (day 10 — offer payment plan if needed) 4. A final notice (day 14 — mention interest or service pause) Each message must: - Not damage the relationship - Offer a specific way to pay (EFT, PayFast payment link, cash) - Be sendable via WhatsApp or email (give both versions) - Include a blank for the invoice number If the client is genuinely struggling, include an option for a part-payment arrangement.

What you'll get

Message 1 (gentle nudge): "Hi [name], hope all's well. Just circling back on invoice #[number] for R[amount] sent on [date]. No rush — please just confirm when you expect to process it. You can EFT or pay via this PayFast link: [link]. Thanks!"

Message 3 (part-payment option): "Hi [name], I understand cash flow can be tight. Would it help to split the R[amount] into two payments — say half now and half in two weeks?"

Why this works for SA

Late payment is a massive problem for SA small businesses. The prompt offers PayFast links and part-payment arrangements, which are culturally appropriate in the SA small-business context.

Growth Tool 1

Turn a voice note into a professional proposal

The problem it solves

A client calls and you scribble notes on a napkin. Now you need to send a proper quote before they cool off — but typing it all out takes 20 minutes you don't have.

Copy this prompt

I dictated a voice note about a quote I need to send. Here's the transcription: [paste your voice note transcript here] Turn this into a professional proposal/quote that includes: 1. A short thank-you opener referencing our call 2. The scope of work in 3-4 bullet points (SA rand pricing if mentioned) 3. Payment terms (include YOCO or PayFast if applicable) 4. A clear "what happens next" section Tone: warm and professional. Not stiff. I run a [type of business] in SA.

What you'll get

Example: You record: "Hey, so Karen from the gift shop in Durbanville wants a website. She's got about 15 products. Needs payment via PayFast. Budget is around R8k. Wants it in two weeks."

The prompt returns a full proposal with scope, pricing in Rands, PayFast link placeholder, and a timeline with Eskom buffer days.

Why this works for SA

SA runs on voice notes. This prompt turns a 30-second WhatsApp voice note into a proposal the client takes seriously. Including "Eskom buffer days" shows you're realistic about delivery timelines.

Growth Tool 2

Write ad variations that don't sound like ads

The problem it solves

You need to run a Facebook or Google ad, but every time you write one it sounds like a bad infomercial. You write one version, it flops, and you're out R500 with nothing to show.

Copy this prompt

I want to run a [Facebook / Instagram / Google] ad for my [type of business] in [city/suburb, SA]. My offer: [describe your offer, price] Target customer: [describe who you want to reach] Budget: R[amount] per day Write 5 ad variations with different angles: 1. The "solves this specific problem" angle (direct, practical) 2. The "here's what other SA customers say" angle (social proof) 3. The "curiosity gap" angle (makes them click to find out) 4. The "limited time / limited stock" angle (urgency without yelling) 5. The "straight talk: here's what you get" angle (no fluff, just facts) Each ad: - Must fit Facebook's primary text (under 125 characters optimal) - Must include a headline (under 40 characters) - Must NOT use words like "revolutionary", "game-changer", or "disrupt" - Should feel like a friend telling another friend about a good thing Also suggest 3 images that would work for this ad (describe them so I can create or find them).

What you'll get

Example for a Pretoria dog groomer:

Text: Your dog is shedding everywhere. We'll fix that in 90 minutes. R350 for a full groom (wash, cut, nails, ears). Includes pickup in the Garsfontein area.

Headline: Full groom, R350, we collect.

Suggested image: Before-and-after of a fluffy dog, "Garsfontein" text overlay.

Why this works for SA

Most SA small businesses waste ad spend on generic copy. This prompt forces suburb-level targeting — how local SA businesses actually win customers.

Growth Tool 3

Turn a client catch-up into a one-page summary

The problem it solves

You had a 30-minute client meeting. Now you need to send a summary, action items, and next steps. You'll do it later — but "later" means the client follows up asking "did you get my email?"

Copy this prompt

I just had a meeting with a client. Here are my rough notes (bullet points, messy, from my phone): [paste your meeting notes here] Turn this into a professional one-page meeting summary with: 1. A 1-line subject line for email 2. What was discussed (3-5 bullet points, plain language) 3. Decisions made (bold these) 4. Action items in a table: Who | What | By When 5. Next meeting: [date if discussed, or "TBC"] Format it so I can copy-paste directly into an email. No greetings or sign-offs needed (I'll add those). Keep it to one screen max.

What you'll get

Subject: Summary: Website project — decisions & next steps | 21 June

Discussed:
- New logo colours: navy + orange (agreed, final version by Friday)
- Homepage layout: client prefers the "story-first" option over grid
- Decided: Go live date pushed to 15 July (waiting on product photos)

Action items: You: Send photos by Mon 24 Jun | Us: Deliver logo by Fri 28 Jun
Next meeting: Mon 1 Jul, 10am

Why this works for SA

SA business owners take calls on the move — in the car, between deliveries, or during load shedding. This prompt turns messy phone notes into a professional output the client takes seriously.

Deep Strategy 1

Reschedule your day when Eskom ruins your plan

The problem it solves

You planned your day around clients. Then Eskom drops a Stage 6 surprise. Your fridge defrosts, your internet dies, and your carefully scheduled Zoom calls are now impossible.

Copy this prompt

I'm in [city/suburb, SA]. Eskom just announced [stage] load shedding from [start time] to [end time]. Here's what I had planned for today: [List your planned tasks] I run a [type of business] from home or a small office. I have a [UPS / generator / inverter / nothing]. Please rewrite my day so that: - Tasks needing internet go in the [available power window] - Offline tasks (paperwork, phone calls, physical prep) go during load shedding - Client appointments are rescheduled with a polite template I can WhatsApp them - I don't lose more than 2 hours of productive time Give me a side-by-side table: original plan vs revised plan.

What you'll get

Example for a Cape Town consultant (Stage 4, 10am-2pm):

8-10am: Batch 3 client calls back-to-back (internet available)
10am-12pm: Load shedding — write proposals offline, organise files
12-2pm: Load shedding — errands, lunch
2-4pm: Internet back — send emails, update CRM

Client reschedule: "Hi [name], Eskom surprised us with Stage 4. Our 11am call won't work without internet. Can we move to 2pm?"

Why this works for SA

Only in SA do you need an AI prompt to outsmart Eskom. This prompt considers UPS vs inverter vs nothing, and adapts to specific load shedding stages — pure South African business reality.

Deep Strategy 2

Get found on Google Maps in your area

The problem it solves

Someone searches "[your service] near me" on Google and your competitor shows up first. You're losing customers who are literally looking for you.

Copy this prompt

I run a [type of business] in [suburb/city, SA]. My Google Business profile exists but isn't ranking well for local searches. My competitors are [competitor 1, competitor 2]. Write me a local SEO action plan with: 1. Five keywords SA customers actually search for (e.g. "plumber Randburg", "bakery near me Fourways") — not generic SEO jargon 2. Three Google Business profile improvements I can make today (most people skip these) 3. A 200-character "Google Business description" that includes my suburb, city, and main service (no hype, just facts) 4. Five review request templates I can WhatsApp to past customers (SA-friendly, not pushy) 5. Two local directories in SA I should list my business on (not Yelp, not Yellow Pages US) Keep it practical. No SEO theory.

What you'll get

Example for a Centurion electrician: "Licensed electrician in Centurion, Pretoria. Domestic wiring, DB board upgrades, load shedding backup installations. Same-day service in Wierdapark, Zwartkop, Heuweloord."

SA directories: SA Yellow Pages, Nic's Local Listings, SA Business Directory — all free.

Why this works for SA

Google "near me" searches are how SA customers find plumbers, electricians, and bakers. Most SA small businesses have a profile but haven't filled in the details — this prompt fixes that in 30 minutes.

Deep Strategy 3

Find what your competitors are missing

The problem it solves

You know who your competitors are, but you can't put your finger on why some customers choose you and others don't. You're flying blind with pricing, messaging, and offers.

Copy this prompt

I run a [type of business] in [city/suburb, SA]. My main competitors are: 1. [Competitor name] - [what they do well] 2. [Competitor name] - [what they do well] 3. [Competitor name] - [what they do well] Here's what I know about my customers: [describe your typical customer, what they value, what they complain about] Analyse these competitors and give me: 1. Three things they all do the same (the "industry default" — this is where I can differentiate) 2. Two things none of them do (a gap I can exploit, specific to SA) 3. One thing I do differently that matters more than I think 4. A pricing observation: are they competing on price or value? Should I? (in SA Rands) 5. Three words my brand should own that none of them claim This is not a generic SWOT analysis. Be specific. SA-specific.

What you'll get

Example for a Pretoria coffee roaster:

They all do the same: Post coffee bag photos on Instagram. Free delivery over R300. Stock photography.

The gap: None offer a "load shedding brew guide." None post their roast date publicly.

Your 3 words: "Fresh. Local. Honest."

Pricing: R185/bag is mid-range. You can go to R220 if you offer the freshest roast date and a WhatsApp subscription.

Why this works for SA

Generic competitor analysis misses local market realities. This prompt forces SA-specific gaps — load shedding adaptation, WhatsApp commerce, local delivery logistics — that national competitors ignore.
